How to Get More Google Reviews Without Being Annoying About It

By the founder of Scrappy Stacks  ·  5 min read

Most advice about getting more Google reviews boils down to "just ask" — which is true, but leaves out the part where asking wrong actually costs you reviews instead of earning them. Here's what actually works.

Timing matters more than the ask itself

The best moment to ask is the moment right after you've delivered something good — right after the job's done, right after the delivery, right after the appointment. Wait a week and the moment's gone; the customer's already moved on mentally, even if they were happy.

Make the ask embarrassingly easy

Every extra step between "yes I'd leave a review" and actually leaving one loses people. A direct link to your Google review page — not your homepage, not a search result they have to navigate from — removes almost all the friction. If you don't have that direct link yet, it takes about five minutes to find via your Google Business Profile.

Don't offer anything in exchange

Google's own policies prohibit incentivized reviews — discounts, gift cards, or anything else offered in exchange for a review, even a "review either way" offer. Beyond the policy risk, incentivized reviews read differently to other customers; people can often tell.

Text beats email for response rate

Texts get read within minutes; review-request emails often sit in an inbox for days, if they're opened at all. If you have a customer's phone number and their consent to text them, a short, direct text asking for a review consistently outperforms email alone.

A happy customer and a reviewed customer are not the same thing. The gap between them is almost always just "nobody asked at the right moment."

What to actually say

Keep it short and specific. Something like: "Thanks for choosing us! If you have a minute, we'd really appreciate a quick review — here's the link: [link]." No guilt, no pressure, no ten-sentence explanation of why reviews matter to you. They already know.

The real fix: stop relying on remembering

The advice above works — right up until you get busy and forget to send it for two weeks straight, which is exactly what happens to almost every small business owner. The businesses with steady, growing review counts usually aren't better at remembering. They've just taken remembering out of the equation entirely by automating the ask.
