How to Become a Mobile Notary Signing Agent

By the founder of Scrappy Stacks  ·  6 min read

Mobile notary signing agent work is one of the more overlooked paths into self-employment — flexible hours, real demand from real estate and title companies, and a genuinely low barrier to entry compared to most licensed trades. Here's how the path actually works.

Step 1: Get commissioned as a notary in your state

Every state has its own process, but most involve a short application, a small fee, and sometimes a brief training course or exam. Some states also require a surety bond. This step alone doesn't make you a signing agent yet — it just makes you a notary, which is the prerequisite.

Step 2: Get your E&O insurance and (usually) an NNA certification

Errors and omissions insurance protects you if a signing goes wrong through no fault of your own — it's typically inexpensive (often under $100/year for a basic policy) and title companies frequently require proof of it before hiring you. Many signing agents also complete the National Notary Association's Signing Agent certification, which isn't legally required everywhere but is widely expected in the industry and helps you get accepted onto signing platforms.

Step 3: Get on signing services and reach out to title companies directly

Most new signing agents' first jobs come through signing services — platforms that connect title companies and lenders with available notaries in an area. Signing directly with local title and escrow offices tends to pay better once you have some experience, but signing services are usually the fastest way to get your first few closings under your belt.

Step 4: Build your actual toolkit

  • A reliable printer (many closings still require you to print the loan package)
  • A notary seal and journal that meet your state's requirements
  • Professional attire — you're representing the closing table
  • A flexible schedule — closings often get requested with short notice
The work itself is straightforward once you've done a handful of closings. The harder part, for most people, is the steady stream of new business once the initial signing-service jobs slow down.

What nobody tells you about the slow months

Signing volume follows the real estate market — refinance booms create a flood of work, and quiet housing markets mean fewer closings to go around. The signing agents who stay busy year-round are usually the ones who've built direct relationships with a handful of title companies, rather than relying entirely on signing-service platforms.

Getting your name in front of people

Like any service business, this work depends on people finding you — a real Google Business Profile, a fictitious name if you're operating under a business name instead of just your own, and a steady stream of reviews from happy clients and title companies alike.
