Reviews & Reputation

I Stopped Asking Customers For Reviews. My Rating Dropped Anyway.

By the founder of Scrappy Stacks  ·  4 min read

Nobody left angry. That's the part that confused me for a while. My rating started slipping, and when I went back through a few months of jobs looking for what went wrong, I couldn't find it. No bad experience. No complaint I'd missed. Just... quiet.

What actually happened was simpler and a lot less dramatic: I got busy, I stopped asking, and the reviews stopped coming in. Meanwhile a couple of competitors down the road kept posting new ones every week. Same market, same kind of work — the only difference was they had a system and I had a habit I'd let slide.

A happy customer and a reviewed customer are not the same thing. Only one of them shows up on Google.

The math I wasn't doing

Here's what I hadn't thought through: your rating isn't a lifetime average, it's a rolling one. New customers weigh recent reviews more than old ones. So a great year followed by three quiet months doesn't just stall your growth — it can actively drag your rating down, because the good reviews from years back start to matter less than the silence right now.

I wasn't losing customers to bad experiences. I was losing the future customers who never showed up, because they searched, saw a rating that hadn't moved in months, and picked whoever looked more active.

Why "just remember to ask" doesn't work

I know the advice. Ask at the end of the job, while they're happy. I agreed with it every single time I heard it, and then I still didn't do it — not because I didn't care, but because the moment right after finishing a job is exactly when you're already thinking about the next one. Asking for a review takes a back seat to literally everything else on your plate that day.

  • You're packing up, not thinking about marketing
  • You feel a little awkward asking directly, even when they'd say yes
  • By the time you remember, they've already moved on with their day
  • Multiply that by every job, every week, for months

None of that is a character flaw. It's just what happens when something depends entirely on remembering to do it in the one moment you have the least bandwidth to remember anything.

What actually fixed it

The fix wasn't trying harder to remember. It was taking "remembering" out of the job entirely. I set up something that automatically emails and texts every customer a few hours after the fact, asking for a quick review — no sticky notes, no mental checklist, nothing riding on whether I'm having a good day or a slammed one.

Within a few weeks the trickle turned back into a steady stream, and it's stayed that way since — not because I got better at asking, but because I stopped needing to remember to.

If you've noticed the same quiet slide in your own rating, it's probably not your work. It's probably just that nobody's asking anymore.
