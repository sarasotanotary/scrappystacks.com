Wave vs. QuickBooks: Which Is Actually Better for a One-Person Business

By the founder of Scrappy Stacks  ·  5 min read

Both Wave and QuickBooks handle invoicing and basic bookkeeping, but they're built for different businesses. Here's the honest comparison, without either company's marketing doing the talking.

Cost is the biggest real difference

Wave's core invoicing and accounting features are free — the company makes money on payment processing fees and optional paid add-ons (like payroll), not on the software itself. QuickBooks Simple Start and similar entry plans typically run $15–$35/month depending on current promotions, with costs increasing as you add users or features.

Where Wave wins

  • Truly free core features — invoicing, basic accounting, and receipt scanning don't require a paid plan
  • Simpler interface — built with solo owners and freelancers in mind, less accounting jargon
  • Good enough for most one-person service businesses — if your books are relatively simple (a handful of invoices a month, basic expense tracking), Wave covers it completely

Where QuickBooks wins

  • More robust reporting — better for businesses that need detailed financial reports for investors, loans, or complex tax situations
  • Wider accountant familiarity — most accountants and bookkeepers already know QuickBooks, which can simplify tax season if you're working with a professional
  • Better scaling — if you're planning to add employees, inventory, or multiple revenue streams soon, QuickBooks' paid tiers are built for that growth
The real question isn't which software is "better" in the abstract — it's whether your business is simple enough right now that free features cover everything you need.

A reasonable way to decide

If you're a solo service business sending a handful of invoices a month with straightforward expenses, start with Wave — it's free, and switching later (once you actually need QuickBooks' extra features) is a lot easier than paying for capability you don't need yet.

If you already know you'll need multi-user access, inventory tracking, or you're working closely with an accountant who has a strong QuickBooks preference, it's reasonable to start there instead.

Sending your first invoice, either way

Whichever tool you pick, the actual invoice only needs a name, an amount, and a due date — everything else is optional polish. The tool matters less than just sending the first one.
