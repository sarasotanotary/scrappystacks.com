How to Respond to a Negative Google Review (With Templates)

By the founder of Scrappy Stacks  ·  5 min read

A bad review feels personal, especially for a small business where the owner and the brand are basically the same person. But how you respond matters more than the review itself — future customers read the response as much as the complaint.

First: don't respond angry

Wait a day if you need to. Google doesn't put a time limit on responses, and a defensive, emotional reply does more damage than the original review — it's the thing other potential customers will actually judge you on.

If the criticism is fair

Acknowledge it directly. Don't over-explain or make excuses. Something like:

"You're right, and I'm sorry we missed that. We've [specific fix] so this doesn't happen again. I'd like to make this right — please reach out at [phone/email]."

This response does three things: it shows accountability, shows a real fix (not just an apology), and moves the conversation offline where it can actually get resolved.

If the complaint feels unfair or exaggerated

Stay factual, not defensive. Avoid arguing point-by-point in public — it rarely looks good to a neutral reader, even when you're right. Something like:

"We're sorry to hear this didn't meet your expectations. Our records show [brief, factual context] — we'd welcome the chance to discuss this directly at [phone/email]."

If you suspect the review is fake or from a non-customer

Don't accuse them publicly, even if you're confident. Instead, respond briefly and factually, then flag the review to Google directly through your Business Profile — reviews that violate Google's policies (fake, off-topic, or from someone who was never a customer) can be reported for removal, though it's not guaranteed and can take time.

What never to do

  • Never argue in the comments back and forth — it reads worse the longer it goes
  • Never share private details about the customer's transaction publicly
  • Never ignore it — an unanswered bad review looks worse than an answered one, even a fair one
A single bad review with a calm, accountable response often builds more trust with future customers than a page of nothing but five stars. It shows there's a real person behind the business who takes it seriously.

The best long-term fix

One bad review matters a lot less when it's surrounded by twenty recent good ones. The businesses that handle bad reviews best usually aren't the ones with the perfect response templates — they're the ones with a steady stream of new reviews coming in, so one complaint doesn't dominate the page.
