What Twenty Years of Nursing Taught Me About Never Working For Someone Else Again

6 min read · The Way Out

I gave nursing twenty years. Double shifts. Missed birthdays. A back that doesn't work right anymore and a bank account that never got the memo that I'd earned better.

Nobody warns you what "giving your career to it" actually costs. You think you're trading time for security. What you're really trading is your body, your family's time with you, and — if you're not careful — every year you had to build something of your own.

I'm not writing this to bash nursing, or any job. I'm writing it because I know there are people reading this who feel the exact thing I felt for a long time: this can't be it. You can love the work and still know it isn't yours. You can give everything and still end up with nothing that's actually yours to keep.

Here's what nobody told me, and what I'd tell anyone standing where I was standing:

You don't need permission to build something of your own. Not a business degree. Not savings you don't have. Not someone else's blessing that this is a "safe" choice. You need one decision and the willingness to start small.

Your body has a shelf life your employer doesn't care about. They'll use what you give until you can't give anymore, then replace you. That's not cruelty, it's just how it works. Which means the only person who's going to protect your future is you.

Owning your time is the actual goal — not getting rich. I'm not chasing a yacht. I'm chasing a life where I decide what my Tuesday looks like. Where a bad back doesn't mean a bad year. Where what I build outlives the career that broke me.

If any of this sounds familiar — if you've given years to something that took more than it gave — I'm not going to tell you it's easy to walk away and build your own thing. It isn't. But it's possible, and you don't have to figure it out with nobody in your corner.

That's what this is. Not a course. Not a guru. Just someone one step ahead, showing the way.
